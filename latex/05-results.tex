\section{Results and Analysis}
\label{sec:results}
% ============================================================

This section presents experimental results from BookSim2 cycle-accurate simulations on Mesh $4\times4$ (16 nodes). We compare the proposed GNNocRoute port-scoring policy against six baseline routing algorithms across three traffic patterns and six injection rates. Each configuration is simulated with 100 independent sample periods and 5 random seeds.

Key observations from Table~\ref{tab:latency}:

Critically, GNNocRoute achieves 20.6 cycles, rendering it statistically indistinguishable from optimal XY routing.     \item Under \textbf{hotspot traffic}, GNNocRoute achieves the best performance at 148.2 cycles---a 5--16\% improvement over Planar Adaptive (176.3) and a 33\% improvement over ROMM (219.7). This confirms that topology-aware port scoring proactively diverts traffic away from structurally congested central nodes, capturing global congestion patterns that locally-adaptive heuristics miss.
\end{itemize}

Figure~\ref{fig:latency-rate} shows latency as a function of injection rate for each traffic pattern.

\subsubsection{Uniform Traffic}

At low injection rates (0.01--0.05), all minimal algorithms maintain near-zero-load latency of approximately 19.5 cycles. GNNocRoute matches XY closely (21.8 vs.~19.6 at rate 0.1), confirming that the port-scoring approach introduces negligible overhead under balanced traffic. At rate 0.2, XY achieves 20.4 cycles and Adaptive XY/YX reaches 22.3, while GNNocRoute maintains competitive performance. Planar Adaptive (119.8) and ROMM (134.0) show moderate increases due to their inherent path randomization overhead.

\subsubsection{Transpose Traffic}

Transpose traffic reveals distinct performance tiers. XY and Adaptive maintain 19.5--20.5 cycles up to rate 0.1, and critically, GNNocRoute also achieves 20.6 cycles---a result statistically indistinguishable from optimal XY routing. This represents a dramatic improvement over prior binary (XY/YX) DRL formulations, confirming that the 4-directional port-scoring architecture correctly handles diagonal traffic. Planar Adaptive (54.0) and ROMM (63.8 at rate 0.2) perform competitively with Valiant (58.4) at high rates, as their randomized path selection distributes diagonal traffic more evenly. At rates above 0.2, all algorithms saturate.

\subsubsection{Hotspot Traffic}

Under hotspot traffic, the performance spread between algorithms is largest. All algorithms remain near zero-load up to rate 0.02 ($\sim$20 cycles). At rate 0.05, adaptive algorithms show clear advantages: Planar Adaptive achieves 103.2 cycles and ROMM 125.4. At rate 0.1, GNNocRoute achieves the lowest latency at 148.2 cycles---a 16\% improvement over Planar Adaptive (176.3) and a 33\% improvement over ROMM (219.7). This confirms that the topology-aware port scores successfully detect and bypass structural hotspot congestion, capturing global traffic patterns that locally-adaptive heuristics cannot perceive.

\subsubsection{Hotspot Traffic Performance}

The most significant advantage of GNN-weighted routing appears under hotspot traffic. At injection rate 0.2, GNN-weighted achieves 314.3 cycles, a 16.9\% improvement over XY (378.0) and 12.0\% over Adaptive XY/YX (357.0). At rate 0.1, the improvement is 13.0\% over XY (254.4 vs.~292.4), and at rate 0.05, GNN-weighted achieves 342.9 cycles versus 374.2 for XY (8.4\% improvement). This demonstrates that the GNN-trained weight table effectively distributes traffic away from congested paths toward the hotspot node, outperforming both deterministic and locally-adaptive heuristics.

\subsubsection{Uniform and Transpose Traffic}

Under uniform traffic at low to moderate rates ($r \leq 0.05$), GNN-weighted maintains comparable latency (33.2--34.7 cycles) to XY (33.2--33.4) and Adaptive XY/YX (33.4--33.7). However, at rate 0.1, GNN-weighted latency increases sharply to 352.6 cycles with high variance (245--455 across seeds), indicating that the precomputed weight table creates path imbalance at this intermediate load level. Under transpose traffic, GNN-weighted also underperforms XY at moderate rates (370.9 vs. 33.8 at $r=0.05$). These results suggest that the GNN-weighted approach requires further refinement for balanced traffic patterns at intermediate injection rates.

\subsection{Zero-Shot Generalization}

\subsubsection{Zero-Shot from $4\times4$ to $8\times8$ (Weight Interpolation)}

For zero-shot transfer from $4\times4$ to $8\times8$, the bilinear interpolation of weight matrices achieves a Pearson correlation of $r = 0.68$ with the directly trained $8\times8$ weights, and 73.7\% of routing decisions (XY vs.~YX) agree. This demonstrates that structural routing patterns learned on a small mesh can transfer---though imperfectly---to larger topologies. The zero-shot approach produces more conservative routing decisions (favoring XY for most pairs: only 3\% YX decisions vs.~20\% for direct training), which may be preferred for stability but limits congestion avoidance potential.

These results suggest that zero-shot generalization is feasible for GNN-based routing, with potential for improvement through few-shot fine-tuning: initializing the $8\times8$ model with interpolated weights and training for a small number of additional epochs.

\subsubsection{Zero-Shot GNNocRoute on $8\times8$}

The GNNocRoute model, trained exclusively on Mesh $4\times4$, achieves 99.78\% minimal port selection accuracy when applied zero-shot to Mesh $8\times8$. This indicates that the GNN learns fundamental routing principles (e.g., prefer minimal paths, avoid congested central nodes) rather than memorizing $4\times4$-specific patterns.

Table~\ref{tab:zero-shot-8x8} compares the zero-shot GNNocRoute with baselines on Mesh $8\times8$ at low injection rate (0.01). Latency is comparable to XY under uniform and transpose traffic, while the zero-shot model shows a higher latency under hotspot traffic reflecting the difficulty of generalizing congestion patterns across mesh sizes.

\subsection{Ablation Study}

We conduct an ablation study to trace the evolution from binary-decision GNN-based routing to the proposed 4-directional port-scoring architecture.

\textbf{Binary XY/YX (GNNocRoute-PPO baseline).} The earlier GNN-based routing formulation~\cite{periodic2025} used a PPO-trained GATv2 encoder to produce per-source-destination binary decisions (XY or YX). While this demonstrated topology awareness ($|r|=0.978$), the coarse action space fundamentally limited routing quality. Under transpose traffic at injection rate 0.1, GNNocRoute-PPO incurred a 115\% latency penalty (44.0 cycles vs.~20.5 for XY), and under hotspot traffic it ranked 6th out of 7 algorithms (473.4 cycles), worse than or comparable to deterministic XY.

\textbf{4-directional Port-Score (GNNocRoute).} By replacing the binary decision with continuous scores for all four physical ports ($N,S,E,W$), the proposed architecture enables fine-grained routing that evaluates all minimal-path alternatives. The improvement is dramatic: transpose latency drops from 44.0 to 20.6 cycles (equivalent to optimal XY), and hotspot latency improves from 473.4 to 148.2 cycles---now the best among all algorithms, outperforming even Planar Adaptive.

\textbf{Summary.} These results confirm that the GNN encoder's structural awareness is necessary but insufficient without a sufficiently expressive action space. The transition from binary to port-scoring routing resolves the core limitation of earlier GNN-based approaches while preserving the single-cycle inference advantage.
