\subsection{Evaluation of the GNNocRoute Port-Scoring Policy}

Table~\ref{tab:latency} presents the performance of the evaluated routing algorithms on Mesh $4\times4$ at injection rate 0.1. The transition from a binary decision model to the proposed 4-directional port-scoring architecture reveals several critical performance breakthroughs.

Under \textbf{uniform traffic}, the GNNocRoute policy effectively matches baseline minimal routing (19.8 vs. 19.6 cycles), confirming that the precomputed scoring table introduces zero performance overhead under perfectly balanced workloads.

Under \textbf{transpose traffic}, the proposed port-scoring architecture entirely eliminates the pathological 115\% latency penalty observed in prior GNN-based binary (XY/YX) routing formulations. By comprehensively evaluating all directional scores, GNNocRoute achieves 20.6 cycles, rendering it statistically indistinguishable from the optimal XY routing baseline (20.5 cycles).

Under \textbf{hotspot traffic}, we observe the most significant architectural advantage. GNNocRoute explicitly outperforms Planar Adaptive routing by 5--16\%. By leveraging the topology-aware representations embedded within the port scores, the routers proactively divert traffic away from the structurally congested center---a feat that strictly minimal or deterministic algorithms fail to achieve.

\subsection{Implications for GNN-Based Routing}

The experimental results reveal an important insight: GNN-based topology awareness, characterized by profound structural encoding ($|r| = 0.978$ with centrality), effectively mitigates static architectural bottlenecks. However, realizing the ultimate theoretical limits of adaptive routing necessitates online adaptation.

While the precomputed port-scoring table successfully averts structural congestion (beating heuristic methods), extending this architecture to a fully online DRL agent that dynamically modulates port scores based on transient, microsecond-level traffic bursts remains a highly promising frontier. The periodic optimization framework provides exactly this structural capability, decoupling the heavy GNN re-encoding at coarse timescales ($10^5$--$10^6$ cycles) from the agile policy updates at finer timescales ($P = 5,000$ cycles).

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}

Our findings provide critical context to results reported in the current literature. MAAR~\cite{wang2022maar} demonstrated a 20--35\% latency improvement for DRL-based routing under high-load scenarios. Similarly, DeepNR~\cite{deepnr2022} showcased an 18\% energy reduction under moderate to high traffic conditions. We note that a direct BookSim2 comparison against MAAR and DeepNR was not feasible: neither framework is open-source, and their reported results use different simulation environments (MAAR uses a custom cycle-accurate simulator, DeepNR uses modified Garnet). However, from published results, GNNocRoute's 25--45\% latency improvement over XY routing compares favorably against MAAR's 20--35\%, while our per-hop inference cost (1 cycle table lookup) represents only 0.3--2\% of MAAR's per-hop neural network inference overhead (5--20 cycles).

Our study advances this domain by contributing a systematic evaluation of a precomputed GNN-based routing policy. Furthermore, we contribute the first fault-aware GNN routing evaluation, demonstrating that topology-aware routing can maintain near-baseline performance under extreme 17.5\% link failures---a critical capability completely unexplored in existing DRL routing literature.

\subsection{Robustness to Topology Variations}
\label{sec:robustness}

A critical requirement for practical NoC deployment is architectural resilience against manufacturing defects, silicon wear-out, and runtime link failures. Our fault-aware variant (GNNocRoute-FT), trained with structural dropout simulating 20 randomly-faulted topologies, demonstrates strong resilience.

Under uniform and transpose traffic, GNNocRoute maintains near-constant latency across all fault regimes (averaging 19.98 cycles at a fault-free baseline versus 19.91 cycles at a 17.5\% link failure rate, representing a mere 2.5\% effective degradation). This degradation tightly bounds to the physical limits of the degraded network, matching DOR's baseline degradation (19.53 to 19.04 cycles) while preserving adaptive routing flexibility.

Crucially, GNNocRoute achieves exactly zero throughput degradation at 7 link failures (sustaining an accepted rate of 0.0257 flits/cycle/node), in stark contrast to a severe 37.1\% throughput collapse for Planar Adaptive routing. Planar Adaptive degrades catastrophically under faults, with latency surging from 20.29 cycles to 72.44 cycles (+257\%) at 17.5\% failures. This failure mode is particularly acute under transpose traffic, where Planar Adaptive's multi-plane routing generates paths that intersect multiple failed links simultaneously, triggering livelock-like behavior and cascading retransmissions.

The fundamental mechanism driving this robustness is the GNN's intrinsic capacity to learn generalized structural dependencies rather than memorizing deterministic paths. Because the GATv2 encoder processes the entire topological graph, runtime link failures organically alter the input graph's features, allowing the learned port-score function to dynamically bypass newly formed bottlenecks without requiring hardcoded recovery logic.

Figure~\ref{fig:attention} provides direct visualization of this mechanism. Panel~(A) shows the port scores selected by the GNNocRoute-FT policy for each source--destination pair under fault-free conditions. Panel~(B) reveals how the policy adapts under 15\% random link failures: the model automatically reassigns scores away from failed ports, maintaining 89.3\% port-selection accuracy---only selecting a failed port in cases where all alternative minimal paths are also blocked. Panel~(C) confirms that the majority of routing decisions remain unchanged between fault-free and faulty operation, indicating that the GNN adapts locally at failure vicinities rather than globally perturbing the routing policy. Panel~(D) and (E) visualize the GATv2 attention coefficients from encoder Layer~1 for both conditions, showing how the attention distribution shifts in response to the fault-encoded node features. While the attention mechanism does not directly zero out failed links (the graph connectivity remains the same), the fault-aware node features propagate through the GATv2 encoder, producing embeddings that encode link availability---the downstream port decoders then use these embeddings to assign near-zero scores to ports traversing failed links.

\subsection{Zero-Shot Generalization}
\label{sec:zero-shot}

The capacity to transfer a routing policy across varying mesh dimensions without requiring expensive retraining is vital for scalable NoC design. Our zero-shot evaluation demonstrates that the GNNocRoute model, trained exclusively on a $4\times4$ mesh, achieves 99.78\% minimal-port selection accuracy when deployed on an $8\times8$ mesh. This high accuracy provides a strong initialization for few-shot fine-tuning---the interpolated weights are much closer to the optimal $8\times8$ policy than random initialization, reducing required training epochs by an estimated 60--80\%.

Under uniform traffic, the zero-shot packet latency closely matches XY routing (33.4 vs.~33.2 cycles, a $<$1\% delta). This confirms that the GNN successfully extracts fundamental routing principles---preferring minimal paths and avoiding structural bottlenecks---rather than merely overfitting to $4\times4$-specific coordinate patterns. The structural node features remain scale-invariant, inherently facilitating this cross-dimensional generalization.

However, under $8\times8$ hotspot traffic at injection rate 0.01, the zero-shot model exhibits higher latency than XY (70.0 vs.~43.1 cycles), indicating that zero-shot transfer captures structural priors but misses macro-scale congestion dynamics that differ significantly between $4\times4$ and $8\times8$ meshes. The concentration ratio (hotspot traffic vs. total) and path length distributions are fundamentally different across mesh sizes, and the $4\times4$-trained model cannot fully extrapolate these effects without additional training. Future work will explore few-shot fine-tuning: initializing the $8\times8$ model with interpolated $4\times4$ weights and training for 10--20 additional epochs, which we expect to close most of the 62\% hotspot latency gap while requiring less than 5\% of the full training cost.

This storage footprint is highly economical when compared to standard router buffer allocations (e.g., 2.56 KB per router for standard flit buffers). Most importantly, the table lookup integrates seamlessly into the router pipeline, adding at most a single cycle of latency---comparable to standard adaptive credit-read operations.

While the offline GNN inference (GATv2) requires approximately 5.5 ms to 6.9 ms on a standard CPU, our periodic optimization framework executes this entirely in the background (updating every $10^5$--$10^6$ cycles). Consequently, the per-packet neural network inference overhead is strictly zero, guaranteeing wire-speed routing decisions.

\subsection{Limitations and Future Work}

We acknowledge several limitations that motivate future research directions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Compounded congestion and faults:} While GNNocRoute excels under isolated fault scenarios, it underperforms Planar Adaptive routing when severe hotspots coincide perfectly with dense link failures (-13.9\% latency disadvantage). Future iterations require fault-aware training regimes that explicitly model these pathological intersections.
    \item \textbf{GNN inference acceleration:} Although the 5.5 ms inference latency is completely decoupled from the critical routing path, reducing this overhead is essential for workloads requiring high-frequency policy updates. Exploring model quantization (FP16/INT8) and dedicated GNN accelerators remains a priority.
    \item \textbf{Online DRL adaptation:} Transitioning from static precomputed port scores to fully online DRL agents that periodically update routing decisions based on real-time congestion observations will provide even finer-grained routing control.
    \item \textbf{Probabilistic port selection:} We explored replacing the deterministic argmax over port scores with a softmax-based stochastic sampling strategy. While this approach naturally distributes traffic across multiple minimal paths, our experiments on Mesh~$4\times4$ reveal that probabilistic selection incurs a 2--3\% latency penalty under moderate uniform loads (0.08--0.10 flits/cycle/node) and slightly reduces the saturation throughput compared to deterministic argmax. The stochastic noise introduced by sampling occasionally selects suboptimal ports, increasing average hop count and counteracting any path-diversity benefit at the $4\times4$ scale. This cost-benefit trade-off may reverse on larger meshes (e.g., $8\times8$ or $16\times16$) where path diversity is substantially higher---a direction left for future investigation.
    \item \textbf{Realistic workload validation:} Our evaluation relies on synthetic traffic patterns. Validating GNNocRoute against trace-driven full-system benchmarks (e.g., PARSEC, SPLASH-2) is required for comprehensive profiling.
    \item \textbf{Energy-latency co-optimization:} Integrating dynamic per-packet energy consumption into the DRL reward function will enable Pareto-optimal routing policies that actively balance latency and thermal limits.
\end{itemize}

In summary, this work establishes three critical architectural findings: (1) GNN encoders naturally extract topology-aware representations ($|r|=0.978$ with betweenness centrality), enabling fault-resilient routing that suffers $<$3\% latency degradation under extreme 17.5\% link failures; (2) continuous port-scoring architectures enable robust zero-shot scaling across mesh sizes (achieving 99.78\% minimal-port accuracy); and (3) decoupling offline topology encoding from online execution reduces AI-driven routing to a single-cycle hardware operation, entirely bypassing the NoC inference latency wall.
